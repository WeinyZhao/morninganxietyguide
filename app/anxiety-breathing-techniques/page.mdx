# 8 Breathing Techniques for Anxiety That Actually Work

You can't think your way out of a panic attack. But you can breathe your way out — in under 60 seconds.

Breathing is the only autonomic function you can consciously control, which makes it the most powerful lever you have to shift your nervous system from panic to calm. When anxiety spikes, your breath shortens, shallowens, and speeds up — feeding the loop. When you deliberately change your breathing, you break that loop at its root.

This guide covers **8 evidence-based breathing techniques for anxiety**, from the well-known to the cutting-edge. Each one works. But they work in different ways, at different speeds, and for different situations. By the end, you'll know exactly which one to reach for — whether you're waking up in a cold sweat, stuck in a work meeting, or preparing for a stressful conversation.

## Why Breathing Works for Anxiety: The Science

Before diving into techniques, it helps to know why they work.

Anxiety activates your **sympathetic nervous system** — the "fight-or-flight" branch. Your heart rate climbs, muscles tense, and breathing becomes rapid and shallow. This is useful if you're dodging a car, but corrosive when it's triggered by an email or a 3am worry spiral.

The antidote is your **parasympathetic nervous system**, nicknamed the "rest-and-digest" system. Its primary accelerator is the **vagus nerve** — the longest cranial nerve in your body, running from your brainstem down through your neck and chest to your abdomen.

Here's the key insight: **you can stimulate the vagus nerve through your breath.** Specifically, slow exhales and breath holds activate the parasympathetic response, literally slowing your heart rate and telling your brain that everything is fine.

The research backs this up:

- A 2017 study in *Frontiers in Psychology* found that slow breathing (4-6 breaths per minute) significantly reduced self-reported anxiety and cortisol levels
- Research from Stanford shows the **physiological sigh** — double inhale through nose, long exhale through mouth — can reduce anxiety in as little as **30 seconds**
- The 4-7-8 technique has been shown to activate the parasympathetic system within the first 3-4 cycles

The techniques below all work, but they work differently. Some are best for morning panic. Others are ideal before sleep. Some build long-term resilience. Choose accordingly.

## The 8 Best Breathing Techniques for Anxiety

### 1. Box Breathing (4-4-4-4) — For Balance and Focus

**Best for:** Daily use, morning anxiety, before stressful meetings

Box breathing — also called square or 4-4-4-4 breathing — divides each breath into four equal phases: inhale, hold, exhale, hold. Each phase lasts 4 seconds, creating a perfectly balanced cycle.

The symmetry of box breathing is calming in itself. It doesn't sedate you or spike alertness — it returns you to center. Navy SEALs use it before high-stakes operations for this exact reason.

**How to do it:**

1. Inhale through your nose for 4 seconds
2. Hold your breath for 4 seconds
3. Exhale through your mouth for 4 seconds
4. Hold empty for 4 seconds
5. Repeat for 5-10 minutes

If 4 seconds feels too long, start with 3 seconds per phase and work up. Consistency matters more than duration.

> 💡 **Pro tip:** Box breathing is the technique we recommend for [morning anxiety specifically](/morning-anxiety-relief). Done right after waking, it interrupts the cortisol spike before it builds momentum.

### 2. 4-7-8 Breathing — The Emergency Brake

**Best for:** Panic attacks, acute anxiety, falling asleep

Developed by Dr. Andrew Weil, 4-7-8 breathing is the most powerful single technique for immediate anxiety relief. Where box breathing balances, 4-7-8 slams on the brakes.

The extended 7-second hold allows CO₂ to build up in your blood, which has a naturally sedating effect on the nervous system. The long 8-second exhale is the vagus nerve's favorite signal — it means "danger has passed."

**How to do it:**

1. Place the tip of your tongue against the ridge behind your upper front teeth
2. Exhale completely through your mouth, making a whoosh sound
3. Close your mouth and inhale quietly through your nose for 4 seconds
4. Hold your breath for 7 seconds
5. Exhale completely through your mouth, making a whoosh sound, for 8 seconds
6. Repeat for 4 cycles maximum

**Important:** Don't do more than 4 cycles when starting. This technique is potent, and overdoing it can cause dizziness.

If you want a deeper dive, see our full guide to [4-7-8 breathing for morning anxiety](/4-7-8-breathing-morning-anxiety).

### 3. Physiological Sigh — Fastest Anxiety Reset

**Best for:** Instant relief, acute stress spikes, road rage

This one's new to most people, but the science behind it is rock-solid. The physiological sigh — popularized by Stanford neuroscientist Dr. Andrew Huberman — is the fastest known way to reduce acute stress.

The mechanism is clever: your lungs have two types of sacs. Anxiety causes the small ones (alveoli) to collapse, reducing oxygen efficiency. A double inhale reinflates them, and the long exhale releases the built-up CO₂ all at once.

**How to do it:**

1. Take a short inhale through your nose
2. **Immediately take another short inhale** — topping up the breath
3. Long, slow exhale through your mouth
4. Repeat 2-3 times

You should feel measurable relief within 30 seconds. It's particularly useful for acute spikes — the moment you receive bad news, after a heated argument, or when you wake up in a panic.

### 4. Diaphragmatic Breathing — The Foundation

**Best for:** Building a long-term practice, reducing baseline anxiety

Diaphragmatic (or "belly") breathing is the technique all the others are built on. It refers to breathing that fully engages your diaphragm — the dome-shaped muscle at the base of your lungs — rather than shallow chest breathing.

Most anxious people are chest breathers. Chest breathing is efficient for fight-or-flight but leaves your lower lungs full of stale air. Diaphragmatic breathing ensures you're actually oxygenating your blood and activating the vagus nerve with every breath.

**How to do it:**

1. Place one hand on your chest, one on your belly
2. Breathe in slowly through your nose — your belly hand should rise, chest hand should barely move
3. Breathe out slowly through your nose or mouth — your belly falls
4. Aim for 6-10 breaths per minute (slower is better)
5. Practice for 10-20 minutes daily

Once diaphragmatic breathing becomes automatic (usually 2-4 weeks of practice), your baseline anxiety drops because your nervous system defaults to a calmer pattern.

### 5. Belly Breathing Variation — For Beginners

**Best for:** First-time breathwork users, anyone who finds other techniques intimidating

If full diaphragmatic breathing feels awkward or you're struggling to feel your diaphragm engage, this simpler variation is the perfect entry point.

Belly breathing is essentially diaphragmatic breathing without the technical focus — you simply focus on letting your belly expand on the inhale and relax on the exhale. No strict timing, no counting, no pressure.

**How to do it:**

1. Sit or lie comfortably
2. Place both hands on your belly
3. Breathe in slowly — feel your belly push your hands outward
4. Breathe out slowly — feel your belly fall
5. Let the breath be natural in pace (roughly 4-6 seconds per breath)
6. Continue for 5-10 minutes

This is an excellent technique to practice [lying in bed before sleep](/breathing-exercises-in-bed), especially if anxiety tends to keep you awake.

### 6. Alternate Nostril Breathing (Nadi Shodhana) — Calming Balance

**Best for:** Mental clarity, meditation preparation, anxiety with racing thoughts

Alternate nostril breathing — called *Nadi Shodhana* in yoga tradition — is a pranayama technique that balances the two hemispheres of your brain while activating the parasympathetic system.

The right nostril is associated with the sympathetic (alert) system; the left with parasympathetic (calm). By consciously alternating between them, you create balance. Research published in the *Journal of Neurophysiology* found that alternate nostril breathing reduces heart rate and systolic blood pressure within minutes.

**How to do it:**

1. Sit comfortably with a straight spine
2. Place your right hand in *Vishnu mudra* — index and middle finger folded toward your palm
3. Use your right thumb to close your right nostril
4. Inhale gently through your left nostril for 4 counts
5. Close both nostrils and hold for 4 counts
6. Release your right nostril and exhale for 8 counts
7. Inhale through your right nostril for 4 counts
8. Close both and hold for 4 counts
9. Exhale through your left nostril for 8 counts
10. That's one cycle. Repeat for 5-10 cycles.

**A note:** This technique is traditionally practiced without counting in the early stages. The counts above are a modern adaptation to make it accessible for anxiety relief.

### 7. Extended Exhale (2:6 Breathing) — Simplest Technique

**Best for:** Absolute beginners, people who hate counting, acute stress in public

If all the other techniques feel too complicated, this one is for you. The 2:6 pattern — inhale for 2 counts, exhale for 6 — is the absolute simplest vagus nerve activation technique. And simplicity wins in high-anxiety moments because complexity requires cognitive resources you don't have when you're panicking.

The key is the **2:1 ratio** of exhale to inhale. Twice as long exhale as inhale is the magic ratio for parasympathetic activation — it doesn't matter if the counts are 2 and 6, or 3 and 6, or 1.5 and 3.

**How to do it:**

1. Breathe in through your nose for 2 counts (roughly 2 seconds)
2. Breathe out through your nose or mouth for 6 counts (roughly 6 seconds)
3. Repeat for 5-10 minutes

You can do this anywhere — at your desk, in a meeting, on the subway — without closing your eyes or drawing attention. In fact, this is one of the few techniques you can do while maintaining a conversation.

### 8. Coherent Breathing (5.5 Seconds) — For Long-Term Practice

**Best for:** Building a permanent practice, reducing overall stress, improving HRV

Coherent breathing — sometimes called " CardioCoherent" — is the practice of breathing at exactly 5.5 breaths per minute (5 seconds inhale, 5 seconds exhale, though most people naturally do 5.5/5.5). This is considered the "resonance frequency" of your cardiovascular system — the rate at which your heart rate variability (HRV) is maximized.

HRV is one of the best proxies for nervous system resilience. Higher HRV means your body can shift quickly between stress and calm. Low HRV is associated with anxiety, depression, and cardiovascular disease.

**How to do it:**

1. Breathe in gently through your nose for 5-6 seconds
2. Breathe out gently through your nose or mouth for 5-6 seconds
3. Maintain this rhythm for 10-20 minutes
4. The goal is to feel no strain — it's about volume, not force

Coherent breathing takes longer to produce felt-results than the acute techniques above. But people who practice it consistently (daily, 20 minutes) report sustained reductions in anxiety and improved emotional regulation over weeks to months.

## How to Build a Breathing Practice (3 Steps)

Knowing techniques is one thing. Building a practice is another. Here's how to turn breathwork from a panic tool into a sustainable habit.

### Step 1: Start with one, not all eight

Don't try to learn all eight techniques at once. Pick **one beginner-friendly technique** (we recommend box breathing or 2:6) and commit to it for two weeks. Once it feels automatic, add another if you want.

### Step 2: Anchor it to an existing habit

The best time to practice is when you already do something consistently. Options:

- Right after you brush your teeth (morning)
- Before you check your phone (morning)
- Before bed, after you get into bed
- At your desk before you open your laptop

Anchoring breathwork to an existing behavior removes the friction of "deciding to do it" — the habit carries you.

### Step 3: Track your baseline and progress

Write down your anxiety level (1-10) before and after each session for the first week. After 2-3 weeks, you'll have concrete data showing what works for you — and a record of improvement that motivates you to keep going.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the fastest breathing technique for anxiety?

The **physiological sigh** — a double inhale followed by a long exhale — works in as little as 30 seconds. The 4-7-8 technique also works quickly, usually within the first 4 cycles.

### How many minutes should I practice breathing for anxiety?

For acute anxiety relief: one session of 3-5 minutes is enough. For building a long-term practice: 10-20 minutes daily produces measurable changes in baseline anxiety within 2-4 weeks.

### Can breathing exercises replace therapy or medication?

Breathing techniques are a powerful complement to therapy and medication — but not a replacement for them, especially in cases of moderate to severe anxiety disorder. If your anxiety is significantly impacting your daily life, please consult a mental health professional.

### Is it normal to feel dizzy when doing breathing exercises?

Mild dizziness is normal, especially when starting 4-7-8 or coherent breathing. It usually means you're overdoing it. Reduce the duration of holds, slow down the pace, or take a break. If dizziness persists, stop and consult a doctor.

### Can I do breathing exercises while lying down?

Yes — in fact, diaphragmatic, belly, and 4-7-8 breathing are all excellent lying down. Just avoid 4-7-8 if you're already drowsy (the sedation can be strong). See our guide to [breathing exercises you can do in bed](/breathing-exercises-in-bed).

### How long does it take for breathing techniques to work?

Acute techniques (physiological sigh, 4-7-8) work within 30-60 seconds. Building a long-term practice (coherent breathing, diaphragmatic) takes 2-4 weeks to shift your baseline anxiety. Like exercise, the benefit compounds over time.

### Which breathing technique is best for panic attacks?

**4-7-8 breathing** and **physiological sigh** are the two most effective for panic attacks. Both work quickly enough to be useful during an attack. If you experience frequent panic attacks, consider working with a therapist trained in cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) alongside breathwork.
