# 5 Breathing Exercises to Do in Bed (And Start Your Day Calm)

The alarm goes off. Your eyes open. And before you've taken a single step into the day, the anxiety is already there — that familiar weight in your chest, the tightness in your stomach, the thoughts already racing.

The instinct is to reach for your phone. To check email, news, messages. To distract yourself out of the feeling.

But there's a better way. And it doesn't require leaving your bed, opening your eyes, or talking to anyone.

These 5 breathing exercises are designed specifically for that hazy, half-awake state — the first 5 minutes after waking, when your rational brain hasn't fully come online yet, but your anxious brain already has. They're ordered from gentlest to most active, so you can choose where to start based on how you feel.

## Why Do Breathing Exercises in Bed?

There are three reasons bed is actually the best place to start:

**1. You're already horizontal.** No balance required. No standing. No "assume a meditation pose." Your body can be fully supported while you do the work.

**2. The cortisol spike hasn't peaked yet.** The cortisol awakening response peaks about 30-45 minutes after waking. If you start breathing exercises *before* that window — literally the moment you notice you're awake — you're intervening before the anxiety peaks.

**3. Physical warmth amplifies the effect.** When your body is warm (from blankets), your parasympathetic system is already slightly activated. The breathing exercises work on top of that baseline, making them more effective.

## The 5 Exercises (Pick What Feels Right)

### 1. Diaphragmatic Breathing — The Foundation

**Time: 3-5 minutes | Difficulty: Easy | Best for: First-timers, chronic tension**

Diaphragmatic breathing (also called "belly breathing") is the most fundamental anxiety-relieving breath technique. It's how babies breathe naturally — and adults forgot how to do it somewhere around age 10.

Most adults breathe shallowly, from the chest. This keeps a reserve of air in the lungs and keeps the body in a mild state of tension. Belly breathing fills the lungs fully and activates the vagus nerve directly.

**How to do it in bed:**

- Lie on your back, knees slightly bent (put a pillow under your knees if it feels better)
- Place one hand on your chest, one hand on your belly, just below your ribcage
- Breathe in slowly through your nose for 4 seconds — the belly hand should rise, the chest hand should stay still
- Breathe out slowly through your mouth for 6 seconds — the belly hand falls
- Keep the exhale longer than the inhale (that's the key)

**Pro tip:** If you can't feel your belly rise, put a book on it. The added weight gives you immediate biofeedback.

### 2. Box Breathing — Balanced and Steady

**Time: 3-5 minutes | Difficulty: Easy | Best for: Daily routine, balanced calm**

Box breathing (4-4-4-4) is the technique on the home page of this site. It's the first one I'd recommend for most people because the equal phases are easy to maintain, even when you're half-asleep.

The name comes from the visualization: each breath phase is one side of a box. You're tracing the perimeter of a square with your breath. This gives your mind a simple, repeatable focus — which is exactly what a racing mind needs.

**How to do it in bed:**

- Stay lying on your back or roll onto your side — either works
- Inhale through your nose for 4 seconds
- Hold for 4 seconds
- Exhale through your mouth for 4 seconds
- Hold empty for 4 seconds
- Repeat

If 4 seconds feels too long, start with 3-3-3-3 and work up over a few days.

**Why it works in bed:** The equal-length phases are forgiving — you don't need perfect timing. And the visual "box" is simple enough to hold in a groggy mind.

### 3. 4-7-8 Breathing — The Emergency Brake

**Time: 2 minutes | Difficulty: Medium | Best for: Acute anxiety, racing thoughts**

If you woke up already in a panic spiral, 4-7-8 is your tool. Created by Dr. Andrew Weil, this technique uses a long, extended exhale to trigger the vagus nerve more powerfully than any other simple breath pattern.

**How to do it in bed:**

- Place the tip of your tongue against the ridge behind your upper front teeth (this regulates airflow — it sounds weird but it helps)
- Exhale completely through your mouth, making a soft "whoosh" sound
- Close your mouth and inhale through your nose for 4 seconds
- Hold for 7 seconds
- Exhale through your mouth for 8 seconds — that same "whoosh" sound
- That's one cycle. Do 4 cycles total (about 2 minutes).

**Important:** Don't do more than 4 cycles when you're first starting. The technique is powerful, and too many cycles can make you lightheaded.

**Note:** This technique is sedating — you may feel drowsy or fall back asleep. That's fine. Let it happen.

If 4-7-8 feels too long, try 2-4-5 first and work up to the full ratio over a week.

### 4. Physiological Sigh — The Fastest Way to Instantly Reduce Anxiety

**Time: 30 seconds | Difficulty: Easy | Best for: True emergencies, instant relief**

This one is different from the others. It's not a continuous practice — it's a single pattern that resets your nervous system almost instantly.

Research from Stanford found that the physiological sigh (a double inhale followed by a long exhale) is the single most efficient breath pattern for rapidly reducing acute stress. It works in one cycle.

**How to do it:**

- Take a full inhale through your nose
- At the top of that inhale, take a short, sharp second inhale (top up your lungs)
- Then exhale COMPLETELY through your mouth — all the way out, empty

That's it. One cycle. You'll feel your heart rate drop within 5-10 seconds.

**Why it works:** The double inhale re-inflates collapsed alveoli in your lungs (they partially collapse during sleep), which increases oxygen uptake. The long exhale removes CO₂ faster, directly reducing the physical symptoms of anxiety. Together, they signal to your body that the threat is over.

**Use it when:** Your alarm goes off and you feel immediate dread. One physiological sigh before you even sit up.

### 5. Progressive Relaxation Breath — Tension Release

**Time: 5 minutes | Difficulty: Easy | Best for: Physical tension, insomnia residue, stiffness**

This combines breathing with a simple body awareness technique. The idea: consciously tense and then release different muscle groups while breathing, systematically loosening the physical hold that anxiety has on your body.

**How to do it — cycle through each body part:**

- **Feet** — Curl toes tight, hold for 3 seconds, release while exhaling
- **Calves** — Flex feet toward shins, hold 3s, release on exhale
- **Thighs** — Squeeze thigh muscles, hold 3s, release
- **Glutes** — Squeeze, hold, release
- **Belly** — Tighten as if bracing for a punch, hold, release
- **Chest** — Fill lungs fully and hold tension, release
- **Hands** — Make tight fists, hold, release
- **Arms** — Curl biceps, hold, release
- **Shoulders** — Shrug toward ears, hold, release
- **Face** — Scrunch eyes and jaw, hold, release

By the time you've done a full cycle, you've breathed deeply 10 times and released tension from 10 muscle groups. This is especially helpful if you woke up feeling "tight" or "stiff" — common companions of morning anxiety.

## Which Exercise Should You Start With?

Here's a quick guide based on your morning:

| Situation | Best starting exercise |
|-----------|----------------------|
| I woke up fine but want to prevent anxiety | Diaphragmatic breathing (3 min) |
| I feel slightly tense but functional | Box breathing (3-5 min) |
| I woke up in a panic or racing thoughts | Physiological sigh first (30s), then 4-7-8 (2 min) |
| I feel physically stiff or sore | Progressive relaxation (5 min) |
| I have 2 minutes max | Physiological sigh (30s) + one full breath cycle of 4-7-8 |

**You don't have to do all 5.** Pick one that matches how you feel and do it for the full time. Over the first week, experiment with all 5 and notice which ones feel most effective for you. Most people end up with 1-2 favorites.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I do these exercises lying down if I want to fall back asleep?

Yes — all of these techniques are safe to do while lying down, and 4-7-8 in particular can naturally lead to drowsiness or falling back asleep. That's not a failure. If you have time before you need to be up, let it happen.

### How long should I do these for?

For maintenance: 3-5 minutes is the sweet spot. Shorter than 2 minutes and you may not get full nervous system activation. Longer than 10 minutes and most people lose focus. If you have more time and enjoy it, there's no upper limit.

### Can I combine two exercises?

Yes. A common combination: physiological sigh (30 seconds) to quickly reset, followed by 3-5 minutes of box breathing or diaphragmatic breathing for sustained calm. Or do one in the morning and one before bed.

### I always reach for my phone first. How do I stop?

The phone is an avoidance tool — you're using it to escape an uncomfortable feeling rather than address it. Try this: do one physiological sigh *before* you reach for your phone. Just one. 30 seconds. Then reach for the phone. Over a week, you may find the urge to check it weakens.

### What if I don't have time?

If you have 30 seconds, you have time. The physiological sigh works in a single cycle. That's the absolute minimum — and it's infinitely better than nothing. Even 3 conscious breaths before getting out of bed is more than most people do.
